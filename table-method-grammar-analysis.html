6. Prepositional Logic & Bucket Interaction

The Prepositional Allocation Law: Prepositions are inherently relational markers. Their mapping distribution is determined entirely by whether they bind to an action (Verb-Bound) or anchor to an entity (Noun-Bound):

  • Action-Bound Allocation (RHS Dominated): When a prepositional phrase modifies or completes the action of the Semantic Engine (ROOT) or serves as an agentive bridge, it strictly gravitates to the RHS block as an environmental modifier or a structural bridge.
  • Noun-Bound Allocation (LHS/RHS Variable): If a prepositional phrase acts adjectival to define or restrict a noun, it loses its independent trajectory and is "swallowed" into that noun's functional unit. Consequently, if it modifies the Subject, it will sit directly inside the LHS block.

The Three Left-Hand Side (LHS) Manifestations

Prepositional phrases can explicitly breach or occupy the LHS subject territory under three systemic conditions:

  1. 1. Subject-Bound Adjectival Modifiers (The Structural Swallow): When a PP restricts a subject noun, the entire cluster maps as a single functional block in the LHS cell (e.g., "The mouse in the house...").
  2. 2. Fronted Environmental Modifiers (The Topicalization Shift): Situational contexts pulled to the absolute front of the circuit for emphasis, resting as a Hanging Left Modifier (e.g., "In the morning, the cat sleeps").
  3. 3. Nominal Prepositional Subjects (The Rare Sovereignty): Spatial descriptions operating directly as existential subjects (e.g., "Under the bed is a scary place").

7. Structural Ambiguity & Prepositional Attachment

When multiple prepositional phrases stack up in the RHS of a standard baseline sentence, they can create Structural Ambiguity. This occurs when it is grammatically unclear exactly which preceding block the prepositional phrase is modifying.

In standard structural flow, an RHS prepositional phrase has two potential attachment points:

  1. Adjectival Attachment: Modifying the LHS or RHS Noun/Object directly (e.g., "The box [of chocolates]").
  2. Adverbial Attachment: Pointing backwards to modify the ROOT Semantic Engine (e.g., "fixed the car [with a wrench]").

When a sentence ends with a modifier (e.g., "The policeman shot the thief with a gun"), the Table Method dutifully maps the phrase into the RHS. However, grammar alone cannot resolve if the phrase attaches to the thief (he had a gun) or the shooting (the method of the shot). This ambiguity is typically resolved via semantic context, movement tests (shifting the phrase to the front), or substitution tests.

8. Post-Discussion Appendix: Types of Grammatical Units

To scale your linguistic parsing beyond single-word tokens, you must track the hierarchical scale of language. In the Table Method, a cell is not restricted to a single word; it is filled by an operational unit acting as a single cohesive block.

Level 1 The Morpheme (The Sub-Atomic Unit):

The smallest core carrier of meaning or structural modification (e.g., the root "cat", or the inflectional marker "-s" denoting plural status). In your system, these are handled internally within words to influence how a component maps (e.g., tracking the "-ed" ending to properly register a modified Bucket 1 Semantic Engine).

Level 2 The Word / Lexeme (The Atomic Token):

The base freestanding entry level in language. In isolated scenarios, a single word can claim an entire cell (such as "mice" populating the RHS or "sleep" claiming the ROOT).

Level 3 The Phrase (The Functional Block — Critical Table Rule):

A cluster of words acting together as a single part of speech, lacking a subject-predicate split of its own. Crucially, an entire phrase moves together and occupies exactly ONE cell in your matrix.
  • Noun Phrases (NP): "The big, fluffy black cat" contains five words, but functionally acts as a single operational identity. It occupies the LHS or RHS as a single, un-splittable block.
  • Prepositional Phrases (PP): "By the aggressive street cat" acts collectively as a modification vector in the RHS slot.

Level 4 The Clause (The Full Grid Circuit):

A structural assembly containing a Subject (LHS) and a structural Verb line (Aux/ROOT). A clause represents a complete iteration of the table method matrix.
  • Independent Clauses: Can stand alone as a self-sustaining sentence ("The cat is sleeping").

Level 5 The Sentence (The Complete System):

The ultimate terminal boundary, consisting of one or more coordinated clause grids that resolve your entire communicative intention.
